Line-level English/copyedit pass on [[uk-adherence-halffull-pot-longform-native|Wendy_HalfFullPot_UK19]]. Scope is language and readability only, not compliance or strategy (that stays [[me|Talat]]'s call). Ranked most important first.

> [!warning] Top issue: the timeline arithmetic does not close
> "Two years ago" (starts tablets) + "ten months, every single morning" (proper adherence) + "eight months ago" (stops) do not add up. 10 + 8 = 18 months, not the 24 months implied by "two years ago." Six months go unaccounted for.

## Findings

1. **Timeline math (internal consistency).** "Two years ago my scan letter said osteopenia... My GP put me on calcium..." then "I took them properly. Ten months, every single morning," then "Then one morning last autumn... That was eight months ago." If she started at the 2-year mark and took them properly for 10 months, proper adherence ends at the 14-month-ago mark, but she doesn't stop until 8 months ago. That's a 6-month gap the copy never explains. Cross-checked against the explicit "Stopped in October" line at the June check-up: that pins "now" to roughly June, and "two years ago" from June is June two years back, same gap. Since "eight months" is the piece's most-repeated number (7+ uses) and "two years" bookends the piece (opens the diagnosis, closes in the P.P.P.S. "if the last two years taught me anything"), the cleanest fix is to raise "ten months" to "sixteen months" everywhere it appears (3 instances, lines 35/39/93 in the source file) so 16 + 8 = 24. Leaves the two load-bearing numbers untouched.

2. **Product paragraph breaks Wendy's voice (British authenticity).** "Goat milk from Dutch pasture herds, gently dried at low temperature, one ingredient... A2 milk, the kind that sits gently on an older stomach." This is the one paragraph that reads like pack copy, not a woman talking to a friend. "Gently" fires twice in two sentences (gently dried, sits gently), which is a claims-sheet habit, not a spoken one. "An older stomach" is a clinical, third-person way to describe her own body. This is the product-reveal moment, exactly where a sharp reader is most likely to clock "this is an advert." Suggest something closer to: "It's goat milk, dried down to a powder, from a small farm in Holland. Nothing else in it. It's called Goatly. I'd read that goat milk sits easier on an older stomach than cow's milk does, and for me that turned out to be true." Cut one of the two "gently"s wherever this lands.

3. **"Point at" should be "point to."** "And in ten months I could not point at one single thing those tablets had done for me." Point at is for literally pointing a finger. The standard idiom for citing evidence is point to. Quick fix, one word.

4. **Borderline personification.** "A calcium tablet is one lone mineral in a lump... with nothing alongside it to help it in or tell it where to go." "Help it in" and "tell it where to go" give the mineral (and the absence of one) agency, closer to personification than plain physical description. It reads as a plain-English science explainer rather than a literary flourish, so this is a flag for a decision, not an automatic rewrite. If it needs to move: "A calcium tablet lands in the gut all at once, in a bigger dose than the gut can absorb in one go, with none of the other nutrients that normally help the body take calcium in."

5. **Two sentences run long right where the copy is explaining something technical.** "A calcium tablet is one lone mineral in a lump, far more than a gut can take in at one time, with nothing alongside it to help it in or tell it where to go" and "Calcium in food arrives the way a body expects it, in small amounts, with everything it needs alongside." Both are the longest, most clause-stacked sentences in a piece that otherwise runs short and punchy. On a phone, in the one section doing actual explaining, this is where a 55-70 reader is likeliest to lose the thread. Split each in two.

6. **Timeline is tight (softer version of #1).** "By the third week my mornings were easier... On bin day last month I took the pot..." If "eight months ago" + "stopped in October" puts "now" at roughly June, then "three weeks ago" (started Goatly) and "last month" (bin day) both land in May. Not a hard contradiction, but starting Goatly, feeling better by week three, and bin day are all being compressed into the same few weeks while reading like a tidy sequence. Worth checking against real dates rather than relative phrases before this runs.

7. **Comma inconsistency after fronted time phrases.** "Two years ago my scan letter said osteopenia" (no comma) vs "Three weeks ago, on a wet Tuesday, I finally typed the question" (comma); "At my last check-up the nurse went down her list" (no comma) vs "At my check-up in June, when the nurse asked about the calcium," (comma). Not wrong either way, just pick one and hold it.

8. **Dangling "they."** "A T-score of minus 1.8. Thinner than they should be, not yet dangerous." "They" has no stated antecedent (bones is never named). Most readers will infer it from osteopenia, but it costs nothing to fix: "A T-score of minus 1.8. My bones, thinner than they should be, not yet dangerous."

9. **Missed echo.** "I began to dread the swallowing itself, that chalky drag on the way down" vs later "No dreading the swallow, because there is nothing to swallow." Same idea, two different phrasings. The piece uses callbacks well elsewhere (wet sand, telling off) so this looks like a miss rather than a choice. Change the first to "dread the swallow itself" to match.

10. **Minor repetition.** "Why the tablet felt the way it felt" repeats felt awkwardly. Tighten to "felt the way it did."

11. **Secondary packaging-voice example.** "Small Dutch company, fixed herd, and between batches there is a wait." "Fixed herd" is supply-chain phrasing, not something a lay reader would say unprompted. Same issue as #2, smaller scale.

## Clean passes

- No em-dashes anywhere in the copy.
- No real wordplay or puns found. A few dead metaphors ("pot hiding," "the same sentence starts up in my head," "my body had been telling me the truth") are all standard idiom, not literary personification, and don't need touching.
- Americanisms: none found. "Surgery" for GP practice, "telling off," "top-up," "the sort who asks," "blocked up" are all correctly and idiomatically British.

## Verdict

Ready with fixes, not ready as-is and not a rewrite. The timeline math (#1) and the product paragraph (#2) are the two that matter; the rest is polish.
