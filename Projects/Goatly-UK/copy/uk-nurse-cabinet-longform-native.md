# I Am Going to Say Something That Nobody Who Takes Calcium Tablets Wants to Hear. I Was a Nurse for Thirty-Eight Years. I Handed Those Tablets Out Every Single Morning. And I Need You to Know What I Found Out.

Your calcium tablet is not going to fix your bones.

Not Calcichew. Not Adcal. Not the citrate your GP switched you to when the carbonate made you constipated. Not the vitamin D they added on top. Not the £40 magnesium supplement you bought from Holland and Barrett because someone in a Facebook group swore by it.

None of it is going to fix this.

I know because I watched it fail. Every single day. For thirty-eight years.

My name is Sarah. I am sixty-four years old. I was a nurse for thirty-eight years. And I owe you something I have owed you for three years.

The one I think about most is Jean.

Jean was seventy-three. She had been on Calcichew for eight years. Took it every single morning. Never missed a day. Never complained about the taste, the chalky texture, the way it sat in her stomach like wet cement. Her notes said "compliant patient." That is the word they used. Compliant. As if doing what she was told was the highest thing a woman could achieve.

Jean was loading her shopping into her car on a Tuesday morning outside the Tesco on Greenfield Road. Heart attack. She was gone before the ambulance arrived.

I was the one who rang her daughter.

I am not saying the tablets caused it. I am not a doctor. But I am saying that after that phone call I started looking at things I had spent thirty-eight years not looking at.

And what I found made me feel sick.

Not all at once. It crept in. A study here. A professor there. A sentence in a paper I had never been shown in training. Not once in thirty-eight years.

But before I tell you what I found, I need to tell you what I watched happen to the women on my ward. Because you will recognise yourself in at least one of them. And that is the point.

Dorothy played piano at the care home every Sunday for thirty years. She played Happy Birthday when a resident turned ninety. She played hymns when we lost someone. Everyone on the ward knew her hands. Strong hands. Quick hands. She had been on Calcichew since she was fifty-eight.

She broke her wrist at seventy-one. Her hip at seventy-three. Fifteen years of calcium tablets. Her GP said: "She is just getting older."

Dorothy does not play piano any more. Her hands hurt too much. She sits in her chair with them in her lap and listens to other people play.

Fifteen years. Every morning. Compliant.

Ray was a carpenter. Forty-one years in the trade. Built his own house, built his daughter's house. Hands like shovels. Never ill a day in his life. He had been on Calcichew and Alendronic acid since he was sixty-five. When he was sixty-nine his scan showed white deposits along the walls of his arteries. His GP said "comes with age." Ray's arteries had been fine before the tablets.

Ray cannot grip a chisel any more.

And then there was me.

I was sixty. My own GP prescribed me Calcichew. Two a day, plus vitamin D. "It is what we do," he said. "The benefits outweigh the risks."

I looked at the box. I thought about Jean in the Tesco car park. I thought about Dorothy's hands. I thought about Ray's arteries. And I thought about the hundreds of women I had watched take those tablets and come back every two years with the same scan or worse.

I could not open it.

So I did what I think a great many women quietly do when their GP prescribes something and something in their gut says no. I tried everything else.

Magnesium. £18 a month. Four months. Nothing changed.

Vitamin K2. £22 a month. Read about it on a forum at midnight. Took it for six months. Nothing changed.

Boron. £14 a month. A woman on Mumsnet swore by it. I believed her. Nothing changed.

Collagen powder. £35 a month. Stirred it into my coffee every morning. Tasted like nothing. Did nothing.

Resistance training with a physio. £40 an hour. Twice a week for three months. She was lovely. My arms got stronger. My scan did not move.

I ate so much kale I cannot look at it any more. I ate so much cheese my husband started hiding it. I drank milk until it made me bloated and I switched to almond milk and then someone told me almond milk has almost no calcium so I switched back and felt bloated again.

I added it up one night.

£2,340.

£2,340 on supplements and appointments and foods that were all doing the same thing: throwing calcium at my body in forms my body could not use. Not one pound went toward understanding why.

And my GP brought it up again at my next appointment. Same sentence. Word for word. "Sarah, it has been three years. Please. The benefits outweigh the risks."

That night I could not sleep.

I got up at two in the morning. I sat at my kitchen table with my laptop. And I did something I should have done thirty-eight years ago.

I did not read the leaflets. I did not read the NHS website. I did not read Mumsnet.

I read what the bone professors themselves actually wrote.

And I found what nobody had told me.

Not in training. Not in thirty-eight years of ward rounds. Not my GP. Not the consultants. Nobody.

The calcium tablet was never meant to be the answer.

I need you to read that again because I needed to read it three times.

The bone specialists themselves. The people who study this for a living. When you look at how they describe a calcium tablet, you find a quiet little word. Adjunct. Top-up. Supporting act.

It was never the treatment. It was only ever meant to stand behind something else. And nobody ever told me. And I never told the hundreds of women I handed those boxes to every morning.

We all leaned our entire futures on the supporting act. Because not one person in the system ever said: this is not the answer. This is a top-up behind the answer.

So what is the answer?

The same professors. The same papers. The same sentence, every time: get your calcium from food.

Not a pill. Not a supplement. Not a compressed chalk tablet. Food.

That was the sentence nobody had ever finished for me. That was what I sat with at two in the morning at my kitchen table with my hands shaking. Because it was not complicated. It was not hidden. It was right there in every paper from every expert.

And the system had skipped it. Every time. For decades.

I cannot explain the full science here. I am a nurse, not a researcher, and I would get it wrong. But there is an article that explains it properly. What happens when calcium arrives from food versus a tablet. Why your body treats them completely differently. What the professors actually said after the headlines that everyone misread.

I will link it at the bottom.

Here is what I did.

I put one scoop of goat milk powder in my morning tea. That is it. Not a tablet. Not another supplement. Not another £35 a month powder that tasted like nothing and did nothing. A spoon in my tea. It dissolved and I drank it and that was the whole thing.

Week one. The constipation I had lived with for years from the tablets I had taken before was gone. Not better. Gone. I did not notice it happening. I noticed it not being there.

Week two. The heaviness every morning. The one I thought was just what sixty felt like. The slow getting out of bed, hands flat on the mattress, waiting for the back to settle, then the knees, then the feet. It started easing. Not dramatically. Just lighter. The way you notice a headache only after it stops.

Week four. I woke up one morning and got out of bed and walked to the bathroom and was halfway through brushing my teeth before I realised I had not done the routine. No hands on the mattress. No waiting. No negotiating with my own body. I just got up.

I stood there with the toothbrush in my hand and I thought: when did this start?

Month three. I went for my scan. My GP looked at the results and looked at me and said: "Whatever you are doing, keep doing it."

He did not ask what I was doing. I did not tell him. Because I knew what he would say. He would say there is no evidence. He would say it is not a prescribed treatment. He would say the benefits of Calcichew outweigh the risks.

And I would think about Jean. And I would say nothing. And I would keep doing what I am doing.

One scoop. Every morning. In my tea.

That is what I do now. That is all I do. And it is more than thirty-eight years of Calcichew ever did for any woman I watched take it.

I think about Jean every Tuesday morning. I think about Dorothy sitting with her hands in her lap. I think about Ray who built two houses and cannot hold a chisel. I think about the hundreds of women who looked at me and trusted me and I handed them the box and said "take it with water, before your breakfast" and moved on to the next patient.

I cannot ring them all. I cannot undo thirty-eight years.

But I can tell you.

Read it before you take another tablet. That is all I am asking.

Nurse (38 years)
