# I Told Women to Take Their Calcium for Twenty Years. Last Year I Had to Sit Down and Ask Myself What I Had Actually Been Telling Them to Swallow.

Compressed chalk.

That is what it is. Calcium carbonate. Limestone, ground down and pressed into a pill. I prescribed it for twenty years, and I never once turned the bottle around and sat with what that actually meant.

I had a patient called Margaret. Sixty-one. She did not drink. She walked. She took her calcium every single day for as long as I had known her. And she broke her wrist reaching for a saucepan.

Not a fall. A reach.

I sat with her results in my office and I felt something I do not think nutritionists are supposed to feel. Shame. Because I had told her the tablet was enough. And it was not. And some part of me had known for a long time that it was not.

So I did something I should have done ten years earlier. I went back to the research. Not the headlines. The actual studies. What Professor Spector actually wrote. What the Royal Osteoporosis Society actually recommends. All of it.

And there it was. In every single one. The same sentence, hiding in plain sight.

"Get your calcium from food."

Not "give up on calcium." Not "stop worrying about your bones." The message was never that calcium does not matter. The message was that a chalk tablet is not how your body was ever built to receive it.

I had been reading those papers for years. I had even cited them in talks. And I had never, not once, finished that sentence out loud to the women sitting in front of me.

I think about why. And I think it is because finishing that sentence would have meant admitting that what I had been recommending for twenty years was the wrong form. And that is a hard thing to say out loud when you are the one they trusted.

So I am saying it now.

The tablet was never the treatment. The bone specialists themselves use a word for it that most women never hear. Adjunct. It means a supporting act. A top-up. Not the answer. I had let women build their entire future on the supporting act, and I had never told them it was only ever meant to stand behind something else.

Margaret's gut had been telling her. The heaviness. The constipation. The way the tablet sat like a stone every morning. That was the calcium her body could not absorb, sitting in her digestive system, going nowhere. Her body had been protesting for years. She thought it was a side effect she had to accept. I told her it was.

I was wrong.

I cannot go back and un-prescribe twenty years of chalk tablets. But I can tell you what I tell women now. The professors are right. The tablet does not work. And the answer is in the very next sentence they wrote, the one nobody ever reads out loud.

Get your calcium from food.
