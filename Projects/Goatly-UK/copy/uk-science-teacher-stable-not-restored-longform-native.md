# My Mother Took Her Bone Medication Every Thursday for Seven Years. Her Scan Said Stable the Whole Time. Then Her Hip Broke Reaching for the Marmalade.

My mother had osteoporosis. She took her calcium every day and her bone medication every Thursday for seven years. She fractured her hip anyway. And I nearly walked down the exact same path.

By the time you finish reading this, you are going to be angry. I was.

Because three things are happening right now, and nobody is saying them out loud.

One. You are a woman past menopause, watching your bone density slip on every scan, doing everything you were told to do. And you can feel the clock ticking toward a medication you are dreading and a conversation you do not know how to say no to.

Two. A system built to manage conditions, not to fix them, is steering you toward a drug that slows the damage but never touches what is actually broken underneath.

Three. There is an industry selling calcium to women like you that forty years of research has never shown to reliably prevent a single fracture. It is probably in a bottle on your kitchen counter right now.

Let me tell you my mother's story. Because what happened to her shows you exactly how broken this is.

My mother was sixty-four when she had her first bone scan.

She felt completely fine. Active. Sharp. Out in her garden every single morning before the rest of the house was awake. She only went for the scan because her GP mentioned it at a routine appointment. A precaution. Nothing urgent.

The result: osteopenia. A T-score of minus 1.7.

Her GP handed her a calcium leaflet and said come back in two years.

Two years later the scan showed osteoporosis. A T-score of minus 2.6.

Her GP prescribed alendronate. A bisphosphonate. The same drug sold under the name Fosamax.

She took it every Thursday morning for seven years and never missed a dose. A full glass of water. Then half an hour sitting upright at the kitchen table, not allowed to lie down, not allowed to eat. She did the crossword in that half hour. Every Thursday. For seven years.

The heartburn came in the first year. So bad she could not lie flat without it burning. She slept propped up on pillows for three years and never once mentioned it to her GP, because her T-score was holding.

The nausea settled in by the second year. She learned not to plan anything on a Thursday before the afternoon. She said she had got used to it.

By the third year there was a dull ache in her jaw that came and went. The dentist could not find a cause. Her GP said it was probably nothing to do with the medication.

She stayed on it anyway.

Because her T-score was stable.

Not improving. Not rebuilding. Stable.

Seven years of stable.

Then one ordinary morning she reached up to the top shelf of the larder for the marmalade. A reach she had made ten thousand times. No slip. No stumble. Nothing on the floor.

Her hip broke before she hit the ground.

Surgery the next morning. Six weeks in a rehab ward. The woman who had been in her garden every morning of her adult life, who had built that garden over thirty years, lying and staring at a ceiling, learning to use a walking frame.

She never went home again.

She was gone sixteen months after that fracture.

And her scan had said stable for seven years.

I was fifty-five when the menopause hit me. Not the slow fade some women describe. The tide going out all at once. And I knew, from everything I had watched with my mother, that this was the moment something began happening to my bones that I could not see or feel.

I rang my surgery and asked for a bone scan before anyone suggested it. I waited four months for the appointment. I sat in that waiting room thinking about the crossword and the garden she never got back to.

The result: a T-score of minus 1.9.

My GP handed me a calcium leaflet. The same leaflet my mother had been handed fifteen years earlier. Same advice. Same supplements. As if nothing had been learned in fifteen years. As if that leaflet had not been the first step on a path I had just watched reach its end.

I sat in the car park and could not move for a long time. Because I knew exactly where minus 1.9 goes if nothing changes. I had watched it go there. Slowly. Across seven years of calcium and Thursday crosswords and scans that said stable right up until that morning at the larder.

I was not going to follow that path.

So I did everything they tell you to do. Everything.

I teach science. So I did not guess. I read until I understood the difference between calcium carbonate and calcium citrate, and I chose citrate for better absorption. Twelve hundred milligrams, split across two doses, timed away from my thyroid tablet. Vitamin K2 as MK-7, the form that stays active for days rather than hours. Vitamin D3 through the dark half of the year. Magnesium glycinate at night, the gentle form. Collagen in my coffee every morning. Resistance training four days a week, with a trainer for the first three months to be sure I was loading the right places. A weighted vest for my morning walk. Four miles a day with that vest on.

A hundred and fifty pounds a month. Thirteen months of not missing a single day.

My next scan: minus 2.2. Worse.

Thirteen months of doing everything right, and my bones were still going the wrong way. I sat in the car after that appointment and thought: I am becoming her anyway.

My GP, who I liked, who genuinely tried, pulled up the scan and reached for her prescription pad.

I looked at her. "I watched my mother take alendronate for seven years," I said. "I watched what happened."

She was quiet.

"I know the side effects are real," she said. "But so is the direction you are heading. Your fracture risk climbs every month you wait."

"Does alendronate rebuild bone," I asked. "Or does it slow the loss."

Silence.

"It reduces the rate of decline," she said.

"So it would do what my mother's did," I said. "Keep the numbers stable."

She did not have an answer for that.

I took the leaflet. I folded it. I put it in my bag. "Give me three months," I said. "I need three more months."

She was not pleased. She went through the fracture statistics again. She mentioned the guidelines. I held firm. Three months.

I walked out of that surgery furious. Not at my GP. She was doing what the system trained her to do. I was furious at forty years of the same advice producing the same outcome. At a calcium leaflet that had not changed in fifteen years. At the fact that I had done everything right for thirteen months and my bones were still slipping. At the fact that the only answer anyone had was the exact drug that kept my mother's numbers stable for seven years while her bones quietly failed underneath.

So I went to the research the way I would set a problem for my students. From first principles.

Not wellness blogs. Not the supplement websites that are paid to sell you the thing they are describing. The actual published research. And I started with a question nobody had pointed me toward.

Not how to improve bone density. I had exhausted that.

I asked: why does calcium supplementation keep failing to prevent fractures in women who take it faithfully for years?

That question took me somewhere the leaflet never did.

Here is what I found, in plain terms.

When you swallow an isolated calcium tablet, it arrives in your gut as a lone mineral. In there it meets other compounds, reacts with them, and turns insoluble. Your gut wall cannot absorb what will not dissolve. Most of it passes straight through. That heavy, blocked feeling so many women know from their morning calcium is exactly that: the calcium your body could not use, sitting there, going nowhere.

And the small amount that does get through arrives in your blood with no instructions. No escort. No direction to your skeleton. So it settles wherever your circulation happens to take it. Soft tissue. Artery walls. Not bone.

Calcium inside food behaves completely differently. When you digest goat milk, the proteins release small fragments called casein phosphopeptides. These fragments have one job: they hold on to calcium and keep it dissolved, and they escort it through the gut wall and into your blood in a form your body can actually use. That is why researchers in Granada measured calcium from goat milk being absorbed twenty-five to thirty-five percent better than from cow milk or tablets. Same mineral. Different vehicle.

And the food does more than deliver the calcium. It brings the whole team with it. Phosphorus. Magnesium. Vitamin K2. Protein. All arriving together, in the amounts your body recognises, so each part knows what to do. A tablet hands you one brick and no builder. The brick just sits there.

Then I understood the part about my mother.

A bisphosphonate does one thing. It slows down the cells that break bone down. That is the whole of its action. It slows the demolition. It does not switch on the cells that build bone back up. It does not carry an escort so calcium can reach the skeleton. It produces a stable number while the real problem, the calcium that never arrives, carries on underneath.

Stable does not mean rebuilding.

Stable means the demolition slowed down while the builders stood idle.

Seven years of stable. A hip that broke reaching for the marmalade.

Then I hit the part that nearly stopped me buying anything at all.

Most goat milk powder is made with high heat. It is sprayed and dried fast and cheap, and that heat destroys the very proteins that make it work. The escort peptides are gone. You are left with a bag that says goat milk on the label, with the same calcium on the nutrition panel, and none of the biology that matters. Same label. Dead inside. You would be buying the isolated calcium problem in a different package.

So I needed something specific. Dried at low temperature to keep the proteins intact. One ingredient, nothing added, no fillers to get in the way. From pasture-raised herds, because what is in the milk depends on how the animals are fed. And independently tested, so I was not taking a company's word for it.

Before you spend a penny on anything for your bones, ask these four questions. Do not buy until the answer to all four is yes.

Is it dried at low temperature to protect the proteins that carry the calcium?

Is it one ingredient, with nothing added?

Is it from pasture-raised herds?

Is it independently tested, with results you can actually see?

Most products on the shelf fail at least two of those. Many fail all four.

The one that passed every question was Goatly.

Dutch goat milk, from pasture-raised goats, gently dried at low temperature into a fine powder. One ingredient. Nothing else. It uses A2 protein, which is naturally gentler on the stomach than the A1 in cow milk. Independently tested. One scoop in my morning tea, and that is the whole thing.

I did what a science teacher would do. I tracked it. My GP agreed to a blood test that measures how fast your bones are breaking down, so I would have a signal in weeks instead of waiting months for another scan. My starting number was high. My bones were breaking down fast.

I added one scoop of Goatly to my tea every morning. I did not stop anything else. I just added it.

Six weeks later I had the blood test again. The number had dropped by more than a third. I had never seen that number move. Not in two years of supplements. Not in thirteen months of the full routine. My GP ran it a second time to be sure. Same result.

At six months I had my bone scan.

Minus 1.7. Up from minus 2.2.

The first improvement I had ever seen.

My GP looked at the screen, then at me, then back at the screen. She checked twice that she was comparing the same machine. She was.

"Whatever you are doing," she said, "keep doing it."

No prescription pad. No bisphosphonate conversation.

I need to be honest with you about one thing.

This is not a replacement for medication in serious cases. If your T-score is below minus 3, if you have already had a fracture, or if your doctor has told you your risk is high right now, speak to them before you change anything. Do not stop a medication like Prolia or denosumab on your own. That can be dangerous. What food addresses is the delivery failure underneath: the reason the calcium was never reaching your skeleton in the first place. For women in the early and middle stages of bone loss, that is where the direction changes.

And here is what I need you to understand about time.

Bone loss is silent. Nothing tells you it is happening. Just a number on a scan once every year or two, showing you the aftermath of a problem that has been running quietly for years. In this country the wait between the conversation and the actual scan can be months. The problem does not wait with you.

Every month the escort proteins are missing from your food is another month of calcium precipitating in your gut before it reaches your blood. Another month of the demolition running while the builders wait for a signal that a tablet has never carried.

The earlier you close that gap, the more you have to work with. Do not wait until you are sitting with a prescription in your hand and nothing left to try.
