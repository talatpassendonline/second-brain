# My Mother Never Missed a Dose in Seven Years. When Her Hip Broke, I Am Ashamed to Say I Blamed Her. My Own Scan Taught Me Who Actually Deserved the Blame.

I need to start with the worst thing I have ever thought.

When the hospital rang to say my mother had broken her hip, somewhere in the panic there was a small, mean voice in my head that said: she must have let it slip. Missed her tablets. Got careless.

She had not missed a dose in seven years.

Calcium every morning. Her bone tablet every Thursday, sitting upright at the kitchen table for the full half hour like the leaflet said, reading the paper, because you are not allowed to lie down after taking it. Seven years. Not one skipped week.

Her hip broke anyway.

It took my own diagnosis, my own leaflet, and my own scan coming back worse before I understood who actually deserved my anger. It was never her. And if you are a woman past fifty who has watched her own results slip while doing everything right, it is not you either.

I need about eight minutes of your time to explain. Please take them. I wish someone had asked my mother for eight minutes.

My mother was sixty-four when her GP suggested a bone scan at a routine appointment. A precaution, he called it. She felt completely fine. She walked the same three miles every morning, out past the church, along the field edge, back by the postbox. She had walked that loop for forty years. Rain did not stop her. Christmas did not stop her.

The letter said osteopenia. A T-score of minus 1.6. Thinner than it should be, not yet dangerous. Her GP handed her a leaflet about calcium and told her to come back in two years.

Two years later the letter used a different word. Osteoporosis. Minus 2.7.

That is when the Thursday tablet started. Alendronate, a bone medication millions of women are on.

The heartburn began in the first year. Lying flat burned, so she started sleeping propped up. She never mentioned it at the surgery. The letters said stable, so what was there to mention.

Thursdays were written off until mid afternoon because of the nausea. She called it her quiet day. It was not quiet. It was sickness she had decided to live with.

In the third year her jaw began aching, on and off. The dentist looked twice and found nothing. Nobody connected anything to anything.

She stayed on it all. Because every letter, year after year, said the same word.

Stable.

Never better. Never rebuilding. Never stronger. Stable. She circled it once with a biro, like a mark she was proud of.

Then one Tuesday morning she reached up for the biscuit tin on the top shelf of the larder. A cupboard she had opened every morning for forty years. There was nothing on the floor. Nothing slipped.

The surgeon told us afterwards that we probably had it the wrong way round in our heads. She did not break her hip because she fell. She fell because her hip broke. It gave way mid reach, before she ever touched the ground.

They operated the next day. Then six weeks learning to stand again between two rails. The bungalow was sold that summer. She lived fourteen more months, in a room with a window that faced away from the road she used to walk.

Every letter for seven years had said stable.

Menopause hit me at fifty-four, fast and all at once. I could not feel anything happening to my bones. That was exactly what frightened me. I had just spent years learning that you feel nothing, right up until the biscuit tin.

I rang the surgery and asked for a scan before anyone offered one. The appointment took four months to arrive. I spent the wait doing what you might be doing right now: going back through my mother's story, looking for the moment it could have gone differently.

The letter said osteopenia. A T-score of minus 2.0. The same word she started with.

And across the desk came the same leaflet. I mean that literally. Same design, same headings, fifteen years apart. As if nothing had been learned in all the years between her first scan and mine.

I sat in the car with the keys in my lap until the windows fogged up. I knew exactly where minus 2.0 goes if nothing changes. I had watched it happen to her, slowly, one stable letter at a time.

I was not going to be her. So I threw everything at it.

Calcium citrate instead of the cheap kind, because I had read it absorbs better. Split doses, timed away from my thyroid tablet. Vitamin K2. Vitamin D through the winter. Magnesium at night. Collagen stirred into my coffee. A trainer twice a week to make sure I was loading the right bones, then a weighted vest for my daily walk once she had taught me.

£160 a month. Thirteen months without missing a single day.

The next letter came back worse. Minus 2.3.

I read it standing in the hallway and heard myself say it out loud to an empty house. Thirteen months of doing everything right, and I am still turning into my mother.

At the follow-up my GP pulled the scan onto her screen and reached for the prescription pad. The same drug. The same Thursday ritual.

I asked her one question first. My mother took that for seven years and her letters always said stable. So what does stable actually mean? Was anything being rebuilt?

She was honest. I will always give her that.

It slows the rate of loss, she said. It does not rebuild.

Then I would end up exactly where she did, I said. Just slower.

Neither of us spoke for a moment. Then I asked for six months.

She did not love it. She went through my fracture risk, the guidelines, the reasons to start now. I do not blame her for a word of it. She works inside a system that hands out what the system has. I folded the leaflet into my bag and left.

The anger did not properly arrive until the drive home. Not at her. At the fact that the answer to my mother failing on those tablets was to offer her daughter the same tablets.

So I went and read. Not blogs. Not the websites of companies marking their own homework. The actual published research, read slowly, looking up every word I did not know.

And somewhere in those weeks I stopped asking the question everyone asks, which is how do I get more calcium. I started asking a better one.

Why does swallowed calcium keep failing women who take it faithfully?

That question opens a door the leaflet never mentions. Here is what I found behind it, in the plainest words I can manage.

When you swallow a calcium tablet, the calcium goes in alone. One mineral, nothing with it. In your gut it meets everything else in there, reacts, and turns into a form that will not dissolve. And what will not dissolve cannot pass through the gut wall. Most of it never enters your blood at all. It simply travels through you. That heavy, sitting-in-your-stomach feeling after the morning tablet, the constipation half the women my age quietly live with: that is the part that never made it.

The little that does get through arrives with no instructions. Nothing tells it where it is needed. Very little of it ends up in bone.

Calcium in food does not arrive alone. When goat milk is digested, its proteins break into small pieces that hold on to the calcium, keep it dissolved, and carry it through the gut wall into your blood. And it does not turn up as one lonely mineral either. It arrives with the rest of the building team: the phosphorus, the magnesium, the K2, the protein. Everything shows up together, in the amounts bone expects. A tablet hands your body a brick. Food hands it the brick, the mortar and the crew.

And then I finally understood the word stable.

Bone is two crews working at once. One clears old bone away. One lays new bone down. My mother's medication did exactly one thing: it slowed the clearing crew. That is all it does. It does not wake the building crew. It does not carry a single milligram of calcium anywhere. The letter says stable because the demolition got slower. Underneath, nothing is being rebuilt.

Stable is not strong. Stable is just weakness happening more quietly.

Seven years of that. And then the biscuit tin.

Most goat milk powder is dried fast, at high heat, because that is cheap. The heat cooks the very proteins that do the carrying. The label still says goat milk. The calcium is still printed on the panel. But the part that made it work is gone. You would be buying the old problem in a nicer bag.

So before I spent a pound, I wrote four questions on the back of an envelope. If a product could not answer yes to all four, it went off the list.

Is it dried at low temperature, so the proteins survive?

Is it one ingredient, with nothing else on the label?

Do the goats live on pasture?

Has someone independent tested it, with results I can actually read?

I went through every powder I could find sold in this country. Most failed two. Several failed all four.

The one that answered yes to everything was a small Dutch brand called Goatly.

This is the whole delivery in one go, in the form a body recognises as food. And the A2 protein sits gently on an older stomach. No bloating, none of that heavy chalk feeling.

One scoop into my morning tea. Ten seconds. It dissolves, I drink it, and that is the entire routine. After thirteen months of a kitchen counter full of bottles, ten seconds a day felt almost like a trick.

I did not want to wait a year to find out if anything was happening. There is a blood test that measures how fast bone is being broken down. My surgery does not offer it, so I paid for it privately. Forty-nine pounds.

Six weeks later I paid the forty-nine pounds again.

The number had moved. The right way. After two years in which nothing I swallowed had moved anything, I sat at the kitchen table with that piece of paper for a long time.

At the six-month scan I sat in the same chair, across from the same GP. She read the letter twice. Minus 2.0. Up from minus 2.3. Back to the number I had started with. The first time since this began that the line had gone the right way.

She did not reach for the pad. She asked what I had changed. I told her, and I watched her write it into my notes. Then she said, see you in twelve months.

Twelve. Not six.

I walked out of that building with empty hands. No leaflet. No prescription. Nothing to carry but my own bag. A year earlier I had sat in that same car park unable to move. This time I just drove home.

And one thing about waiting.

Bone loss makes no sound. There is no ache that warns you, nothing to feel, just a letter every year or two describing what has already happened. The waiting list between the conversation and the scan runs months in this country. The loss does not pause while you queue. Whatever your bones are missing, they are missing it this morning, and tomorrow morning, and every morning you spend deciding.

My mother walked her three miles every morning for forty years. Out past the church, along the field edge, back by the postbox. A cupboard shelf ended that.

Last month I started walking her loop. I am fifty-seven, about the age she was when someone first handed her the leaflet. I walk it thinking about her tablets. Seven years of trust, and nobody ever checked whether any of it was actually arriving. And I notice the small things now. The way I take the stairs without holding on. The way I reach the top shelf without thinking about her.

Nobody ever finished the story for my mother.
