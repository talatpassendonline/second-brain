# Can Your Body Actually Absorb a Calcium Tablet?

I used to tell women to just push through the constipation on their calcium tablets. Drink more water. Give it three weeks. It'll settle down.

Then it was me, sat on my own toilet at half six in the morning before an early shift, because I hadn't been properly in six days.

My T-score went from minus 2.3 to minus 2.5 on the tablets my own GP prescribed me. On what I take instead now, my last scan said minus 2.0. My GP told me there's no reason to start me on the osteoporosis drugs.

I'm an osteoporosis specialist nurse. Nineteen years running an NHS fracture liaison clinic. Here's what actually happened.

For nineteen years I sat on the other side of the desk. I showed women their DEXA scans. I pointed at the screen and explained what a T-score means. Why minus 1 to minus 2.5 is osteopenia, and anything below that is osteoporosis. I told them, get your calcium from your food where you can. A tablet is only ever a top-up.

A few years ago I sat in the scanner myself.

Minus 2.3. Osteopenia, heading the wrong way. My GP wrote me up for Calcichew D3 Forte, two a day, with food. I picked the box up from the chemist that same afternoon.

Calcium carbonate. The exact tablet I'd handed leaflets about to other women for two decades.

The first few weeks, my body started telling me things I'd heard from patients for years and quietly not quite believed.

The constipation crept in within a fortnight. Properly seized up. I did the prune juice and the hot water first thing, same as I'd told a hundred women to do. It barely touched it. I started planning my mornings around whether I'd been or not.

By the second month I felt like I was carrying a stone under my ribs by four in the afternoon. My clothes fit differently at the end of a shift than they did at the start of it.

By the third month I dreaded the tablets themselves. That thick, chalky coating stuck to the roof of my mouth. I'd stand at the kitchen sink chasing them down with tea, gagging a little, every single morning.

There was a flat, heavy tiredness underneath all of it. Not sleepy tired. Drained tired. The sort where you sit in the car outside your own house for ten minutes because you can't face going in and having a conversation.

I stopped going swimming with my sister on a Thursday. I stopped going to book club. I stopped offering to have my granddaughter overnight, because I didn't trust my own stomach by morning.

I asked my GP about it. She said give it time, your body will adjust.

It didn't.

At my next scan, fourteen months in, my T-score had moved to minus 2.5. Worse, not better. Right on the line. Below that line is osteoporosis, and osteoporosis usually means the next tablet.

I'd watched patients walk that road for nineteen years. Calcium first, then the bisphosphonates, Alendronic acid, sitting bolt upright for thirty minutes on an empty stomach because lying down too soon can burn your gullet. The jaw worries. Then, if that doesn't hold the line, the injections.

I was terrified of stepping onto that road. I'd sat across the desk from enough women already on it.

So I tried to fix it myself first. My GP moved me onto AdCal D3. Same story, different box. Then I bought AlgaeCal myself, nearly forty pounds a month out of my own pocket. My stomach settled slightly. My tiredness didn't. Three months in, nothing had really changed.

My next scan was still a few months off. If my T-score hadn't held by then, my GP and I were going to talk about starting Alendronic acid.

I started reading reviews on the Boots website at eleven at night, looking for anyone who'd found a way through it. I found two Facebook groups for women on osteoporosis tablets and read every post going back months.

Here's the bit nobody says out loud. Every group has someone telling you the tablets are fine, you just need to push through. So you start thinking you're the problem. Not the tablet. You.

I was a nurse who couldn't leave the house before eleven without knowing where the nearest loo was. Who told my patients to give it time while I necked prune juice in the clinic toilets between appointments.

I started wondering if I was just being dramatic. Weak, even.

Nobody tells you you're allowed to say it isn't working.

I nearly gave up and just accepted it. Maybe this was what fifty-six looked like. Maybe everyone's stomach was like this and nobody said so.

But nineteen years as a nurse teaches you one thing. When something isn't working, it's usually not that the science is wrong. It's that the form, the dose, or the delivery is wrong.

So I stopped asking whether calcium works. Of course it works. That's not in question.

I started asking why THIS calcium wasn't working. For me. In my gut.

I booked a double appointment at the surgery and told my GP everything: the constipation, the tiredness, the twelve weeks before we'd talk about Alendronic acid. I asked if I could try getting more calcium from food instead, alongside what she'd prescribed, and track it properly with bloods and my next scan.

She said yes. She was clear. Don't stop the tablets. Add this in. We track it together. Any change to what I'm prescribed is her call, based on the numbers.

That's the only way I'd tell anyone to do this. GP in the loop. Bloods and scans doing the talking. Nobody just stopping their tablets on my say-so.

What I found out next made me properly angry.

A tablet like Calcichew or AdCal is calcium carbonate. Ground limestone. Chalk, basically. Your gut absorbs maybe a fifth of what's in it. The rest passes straight through you, and some of what you do absorb doesn't even reach your bones, it ends up in the wrong tissue instead.

But that's not even the main problem.

Calcium can't get into bone on its own. It needs something to carry it there. In real food, in milk, that carrier is a protein, and the calcium arrives with the whole team it actually needs alongside it: phosphorus, magnesium, potassium, vitamin K2. Not one lone mineral rattling round by itself.

A dry tablet has no carrier. It's just chalk. It has nowhere to go and nothing to travel with.

And the label just says calcium. It doesn't tell you it's carbonate. It doesn't tell you it's travelling alone.

I went back through research I'd half remembered from my training. The Royal Osteoporosis Society's own guidance says get your calcium from food first, a tablet is an adjunct, never the treatment. A huge review in 2017 looked at calcium and vitamin D tablets across tens of thousands of people and found they don't reliably prevent fractures.

For the first time in months I felt something other than tired. I felt hopeful.

Because if the problem was the form, not the mineral, then there had to be a better way to actually get it in.

I started looking at proper food sources of calcium with an intact carrier, rather than another tub of powder. One name kept turning up in the forums and the reviews, from women posting actual scan results, not just opinions. Goatly.

Pure Dutch goat's milk powder. One ingredient, nothing else in it. Dried at a low temperature, so the carrier proteins survive instead of being cooked out the way a lot of processing does. A2 milk, which tends to sit easier on an older stomach than cow's milk.

My next scan was twelve weeks away. I ordered a pouch.

First scoop was a Tuesday morning. Stirred into my tea, same as I have every morning of my adult life. Expected nothing.

By the end of the first week the bloating had eased. I put it down to coincidence.

By week two I'd been to the loo normally, properly, every single day. No prune juice, no hot water ritual. I mentioned it to a colleague because I couldn't quite believe it.

By week four the morning stiffness in my hands and my lower back had eased right off. The tiredness lifted properly around week six. I noticed because I said yes to a swim with my sister without thinking twice about it.

My GP started tracking my bloods properly. There is a marker called CTX that shows how fast your bone is breaking down, one of the few things that moves quickly enough to actually see. Mine was 0.89 to start. Six weeks in, 0.71. By twelve weeks, 0.58. The marker for new bone being built had gone up over the same weeks. My GP said she doesn't often see both move together like that.

Then my scan came round.

I sat in the same machine I've put a hundred patients through. My GP called me in and pulled the images up next to my last two scans.

Minus 2.3 at diagnosis. Minus 2.5 on the tablets, right on the edge of osteoporosis. And on this scan, minus 2.0.

She looked at the screen, then at me. Asked what I'd changed.

I told her.

She said, I don't see any reason to put you on Alendronic acid. Let's keep doing exactly what you're doing and recheck in a year.

A year on from that appointment, I'm still having Goatly every morning. I came off the calcium tablets completely, with her sign-off, once a second scan confirmed the first wasn't a fluke. No more chalky mornings at the kitchen sink. No more mapping my day around a toilet. I sleep properly. I swim with my sister on a Thursday again. I'm back at book club. I have my granddaughter overnight without lying awake worrying about the morning.

If you've been told to take your calcium and it hasn't done what you hoped, I don't think that's you being dramatic. I don't think your bones are a lost cause.

I think it's probably the form. A chalky tablet with nothing to carry it was never going to behave like food does.

If you're on calcium tablets and you're fed up with the side effects and a T-score that won't shift, talk to your GP about adding a food-based source alongside what you're taking. Mine was glad I asked, and the numbers did the talking from there.
