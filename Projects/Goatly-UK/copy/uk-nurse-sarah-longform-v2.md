# I've Been a Nurse for 38 Years. And I've Refused the Calcium Pills My GP Has Prescribed Me for the Last Four Years.

The one that haunts me most was Jean.

Jean was 73. Eight years on Calcichew. Took it faithfully, every morning, as prescribed. "Compliant patient," her notes said. Compliant.

She was loading her shopping into her car on a Tuesday morning outside the Tesco on Greenfield Road. Heart attack. She was gone before the ambulance reached her.

I was the one who rang her daughter.

That was four years ago. After that phone call, I started looking at things I had never looked at before. Not the numbers on the report. At what was actually happening to the people behind the numbers.

Ray was a carpenter. Forty-one years in the trade. Built his own house, built his daughter's house. Hands like shovels, never ill a day in his life. He had been on Calcichew and Alendronic acid since he was sixty-five. When he was sixty-nine he came in with a scan. White deposits along the walls of his arteries. His GP said "comes with age." Ray was sixty-nine. He had been swallowing calcium tablets for four years. His arteries had been fine before that.

Dorothy played piano at the care home every Sunday for thirty years. Everyone on the ward knew her. She was the one who played Happy Birthday when a resident turned ninety and the one who played hymns when we lost someone. She broke her wrist at seventy-one. Her hip at seventy-three. She had been on Calcichew since she was fifty-eight. Fifteen years of calcium tablets. She does not play piano any more. Her hands hurt too much. When I asked her GP about the two fractures, he said: "She is just getting older."

That sentence. I have heard it more times than I can count. "He is just getting older." "She is just getting older." It is what they say when they do not want to look at what is actually in front of them.

I thought about Ray's arteries and Dorothy's hands every time I opened the medications cabinet. But I did what nurses do. I dispensed what was prescribed and I moved on to the next patient.

Then it happened to me. I was sixty. My own scan started slipping. My GP prescribed Calcichew. Two a day. Plus vitamin D.

"It is what we do," he said. "The benefits outweigh the risks."

I smiled politely and said I would think about it.

I drove home and sat in my car in the driveway for a long time. I thought about Jean in the Tesco car park. I thought about Ray, who cannot grip a chisel any more. I thought about Dorothy, who played Happy Birthday for thirty years and now sits in her chair with her hands in her lap.

I was not going to take it.

But I did not know what to do instead. So I did what a lot of women quietly do when they are frightened and have nowhere to turn. I tried everything. Magnesium. Vitamin K2. Boron. Collagen. Resistance training with a physio who cost me forty pounds an hour. I ate so much kale I cannot look at it any more. I read every forum, every article, every thread where a woman my age was asking the same question I was asking.

None of it moved the number on my scan. And the constipation from the years of tablets before was still there, like a ghost that would not leave.

My GP brought it up again at my next appointment. "Sarah, it has been three years. Please. The benefits outweigh the risks." The same sentence. Word for word.

That night I could not sleep. I got up at two in the morning and sat at my kitchen table with my laptop. And I did something I should have done thirty-eight years ago. I did not read the leaflets. I did not read the NHS website. I read what the bone specialists themselves actually wrote.

And I found one word that stopped me cold.

Adjunct.

That is what they call a calcium tablet. Not a treatment. Not a solution. An adjunct. A top-up. A supporting act. It was never meant to be the answer on its own.

Every woman I had ever looked after, every woman who had sat in front of me and said "I take my calcium every day and I still feel like my body is failing me," had leaned her entire future on the supporting act. Because nobody, including me, had ever told her it was only ever meant to stand behind something else.

I sat with that for a long time.

Then I read the professors. Spector. The Royal Osteoporosis Society. The headlines I had seen for years: calcium supplements do not work. Calcium tablets are useless. A waste of money.

I had always read those headlines and felt a quiet dread. Because if the experts were right, then what was left? What do you tell a woman of sixty-three who is watching her mother's story repeat?

But I had never read the next sentence. The one the headlines never include. And there it was, in every single paper, from every single expert:

"Get your calcium from food."

They were never saying calcium does not matter. They were never saying give up. They were saying a compressed chalk tablet, limestone ground into a pill, is not how your body was built to receive it.

That was the sentence nobody had ever finished for me. Not my tutors in 1988. Not my GP. Not in thirty-eight years of working in the NHS. Not once.

The tablet failed. Calcium did not.

I thought about all the women I had watched swallow those tablets. The ones who told me about the heaviness in their stomachs. The ones who said it felt like a stone sitting in their gut every morning. The ones who told me the constipation was unbearable and I said, "That is normal. It is just how calcium tablets are." I said that. To their faces. For years.

It was not normal. It was the calcium their bodies could not absorb, sitting in their guts, going nowhere. Their bodies had been telling them the whole time. And I had told them to keep going.

I cannot undo that.

But I can tell you what I found next. Because the same experts who said the tablet does not work also said what does. Food. Calcium from food arrives the way your body is built to take it. Not a huge dose of chalk all at once. Small, steady amounts, carried alongside the things that help it actually get used. Phosphorus. Magnesium. Protein. The complete team, in one package.

Not a pill. Not another supplement. Food.

One scoop of goat milk powder in my morning tea. That is what I do now. One ingredient. Dutch goat milk, gently dried. No tablet, no chalk, no stone in my stomach.

I am not going to tell you it is a miracle. I have had my fill of miracles that did nothing. But I will tell you this: the constipation I had lived with for years was gone within a week. The heaviness every morning was gone. And for the first time since Jean's phone call, I feel like I am doing something that makes sense instead of something I was told to do.

And I think about every woman I told to keep taking it and come back in two years.
