# 5 signs your body is following your mother's pattern

I wrote this for the woman who catches herself doing something her mother used to do. Not the big things. The small ones. The ones you laugh off and then go quiet about.

1. You reach for the banister before you need it

Not because your legs are weak. Because something in you says: just in case. You remember when your mother started doing that. First at the top of the stairs. Then at every kerb. Then someone had to hold her arm in the car park. It didn't start with a fall. It started with a hand on the railing.

2. Your clothes fit differently but the scales haven't moved

Same weight you were five years ago. But your trousers drag at the heel and your tops pull tight across the middle. You stopped tucking things in. You started buying longer jumpers. Your mother's wardrobe shifted the same way. You thought she'd put on weight. She hadn't. Her frame had changed.

3. You hesitate before you lift

Your grandchild reaches up. A suitcase needs carrying down the stairs. A full kettle wants pouring. You pause. Not because it's heavy. Because something tells you to be careful. Your mother started saying "careful" to herself around the same age. You remember thinking she was being dramatic. Now you understand she wasn't.

4. Someone mentions you look shorter

Your daughter stands next to you and you notice she didn't grow. You shrank. Or you see a photo from three years ago and your posture was different. Straighter. More open. You hadn't noticed it happening because it happens slowly. Your mother lost four inches between sixty and seventy-five. You remember how her back rounded, how her chin dropped forward, how she stopped looking up when she walked.

5. You say "I'm just getting older" and you don't quite believe it

You say it to your friend on the walk. You say it when your knees creak getting out of the car. You say it when the jar won't open. And each time there's a half-second where you hear your mother's voice saying the same thing. And you remember what happened after she stopped saying it. She stopped trying.

Here's what I wish someone had told my mother.

These five things aren't ageing. They're signals. Your spine is quietly losing height. Your vertebrae are thinning. Your frame is compressing. And nobody told you, because nobody measured it, because nobody thought to check until something broke.

After menopause, your body pulls calcium out of your bones faster than it puts it back in. The vertebrae go first because they're the softest. Your torso shortens. Your back rounds. Your middle pushes forward. That's why your clothes fit differently on the same weight.

My mother didn't know. Her GP never connected the dots. She just got shorter and rounder and quieter about it until she broke her hip at sixty-nine.

I found out at fifty-six, standing against a doorframe and realising I was nearly an inch below the pencil mark from three years earlier. My daughter noticed before I did.

A nutritionist explained something that changed everything: the calcium tablets I'd been offered wouldn't fix this. Not because calcium isn't important, but because a tablet delivers calcium without the rest of the team your bones need to actually use it. Bricks without mortar.

Within two weeks, my hands were easier in the morning. By six weeks, I'd stopped reaching for the banister on the way down. And after three months I stood against the doorframe again, pencil ready.

The new mark sat exactly on the old one. Not a millimetre lower. I will not get that inch back, and nobody honest will promise you that. But the sinking has stopped. That was the morning I stopped calling it my age.
