# I kept telling myself it was just getting older. Then my daughter stood next to me.

There is a photo from my youngest's birthday last spring that I still have not deleted, even though I cannot really look at it.

We are standing side by side at the kitchen counter. And for the first time in my life, she is taller than me. Not by a little. She looked down at me, laughed, and said, "Mum, when did you get so small?"

I laughed too. I said something about flat shoes. But that night, after everyone had gone, I stood in the bathroom and I could not stop thinking about it. Because she was right. My trousers had been dragging on the floor for a while. I had been taking them up, blaming the shop. I had a cardigan I loved that suddenly sat wrong on me. I told myself it had shrunk in the wash.

I was 56. And I had decided, without ever really deciding, that this was simply what 56 looked like.

That is the thing nobody warns you about. It does not arrive as a diagnosis or a dramatic morning. It arrives as a hundred small adjustments you make without noticing. You start holding the bannister. You think twice before you kneel down to the bottom drawer. You wake up and your body takes a minute to agree to move, and you call it "my age" and you carry on. I had a whole new sentence I said to myself a dozen times a day. *Careful.* Careful on the stairs. Careful on the wet path. Careful lifting the grandbaby.

I thought I was just being sensible. I thought everyone my age felt like this.

And here is what I did not understand until much later, the part that still makes my chest tighten when I think about it.

It was never really about the inch. Losing half an inch of height does not ruin your life. What was actually happening was that I was slowly becoming the woman I had spent my whole adult life quietly dreading I would become. My mother.

I watched my mother fold in on herself. I watched her get shorter and rounder in the back and slower on her feet, until the day came that she could not get up from the garden without help and stopped going out there at all. By the end she was frightened of her own front step. And the worst part, the part I never said out loud to anyone, was that she had done everything the doctors told her. She took her calcium every single day. It still happened. So I had filed it away as fate. As something in the family. As the thing that was simply coming for me too, the way it came for her.

So when my daughter said "when did you get so small," what she really did, without knowing it, was hold up a mirror. And the woman looking back had started to stand the way my mother stood.

I did not have words for any of this at the time. I just felt a low, constant unease that I could not name, and I covered it up with longer cardigans and the word "careful."

The moment I am least proud of came that winter. My little granddaughter, three years old, lifted up her arms at the bottom of the stairs and asked me to carry her up to bed, the way I always had. And I heard myself say no. I told her Grandma would walk up beside her instead, and she was happy as anything about it in a second. I was not. I stood at the foot of those stairs and felt something I had no name for then. A kind of grief with no funeral, for a version of myself that was quietly slipping away while I kept making excuses for her. I had not lost my health, not really. But I had started to lose my nerve, and with it the small ordinary moments that make you feel like you, and I was telling absolutely no one.

It might have stayed that way for years if it were not for Sue.

Sue is a friend from my walking group, two years older than me, and one Saturday on the towpath she went up a steep muddy bank like it was nothing. I came up behind her with my heart going and my hand out for a branch, and at the top I said, half joking, "How are you not terrified doing that at our age?"

She gave me an odd look. She said, "Why would I be terrified?"

And it stopped me. Because I genuinely could not answer her. I had assumed we were both quietly negotiating with the same fragile body. But she was not. She moved through the world the way I used to, before I started saying "careful." Same age. Same hills. Completely different.

I could not let it go. A few weeks later I was at the surgery for something else entirely, and the nurse measured my height as a matter of routine. She paused. She looked at the screen, then at her notes from a couple of years before, and she said, gently, the sentence that changed everything.

She said, "You have lost a bit of height. That is worth keeping an eye on."

I asked her what she meant. And she explained it to me in the plainest way, the way I wish someone had explained it twenty years earlier.

She told me that for most of our lives, our bones are not solid and finished like a rock. They are alive. The body is constantly taking a little old bone away and laying a little new bone down, and a woman's oestrogen is the thing that keeps those two in balance. Then the change comes, oestrogen drops, and the balance tips. The body keeps taking bone away, but it slows right down on laying new bone back. And most of us, she said, are simply not feeding our bones enough of what they need to keep up.

So for years, quietly, with no pain and no warning, the inside of your bones gets a little less dense. A little more like honeycomb. It does not hurt. There is no symptom. The very first thing you actually notice on the outside, she said, is exactly what I had been hiding under longer cardigans.

You get a bit shorter.

Because the small bones stacked up your spine are the first to give, and as they very slightly compress, your height drops and your back rounds and your middle pushes forward. The lost inch is not your posture. It is not your shoes. It is the first visible report from the inside.

I went home and I did something that I would tell every woman my age to do tonight. I found the doorframe in the hall where my husband had pencilled all our heights years ago. I stood against it. And the line was sitting well below the little mark with my name next to it. Nearly an inch below.

I stood there for a long time.

Because in that moment two things collapsed at once. The first was the story I had told myself, that this was just my age and there was nothing to be done. And the second was the bigger one. My mother had not been undone by her genes after all. She had been undone by years of a quiet shortfall that nobody flagged for her, while she dutifully swallowed her calcium tablets believing she was covered. The thing I was frightened of inheriting was not in my blood. It was in my bones, and it was being driven by what I was, or was not, feeding them.

Which led me to the question I could not shake. If she took her calcium every day and it still happened, then what on earth had been the point of the tablet?

I asked the nurse exactly that on my next visit. And what she told me reframed the whole thing.

She said the trouble with a calcium tablet is that it tries to deliver a great slug of one mineral all at once, and the body can only actually take in a small amount of calcium at a time. The rest tends to sit in the gut, which is why so many women find those tablets bind them up or turn their stomach, and then it simply passes through. On top of that, she said, calcium does not work alone. Bone is built by a team, calcium together with the other things it naturally arrives with in food. A lone tablet is a bit like dropping a pallet of bricks at a building site with no mortar and no builders. The bricks just sit there.

Then she said the line that genuinely surprised me. She said the guidance has always been to get your calcium from your food first, that food is the form the body is built to actually use, and that a tablet was only ever meant to be a top-up, not the answer. I had spent years with it backwards. I had treated the tablet as the hero and my food as an afterthought.

That was the thing that sent me looking for a simpler way to get real, food-form calcium into my body every single day. Not another tablet I would forget on the windowsill within a fortnight. Something that was actually food. Something gentle enough that I would genuinely keep it up, because the bones do not care what you do once, they care what you do every morning for years.

By the spring, I was back down on my knees in the garden planting out, and I got up again without doing the mental risk assessment I used to. My husband was the one who said it out loud. He said, "You move like you used to."

And that spring, at the bottom of those same stairs, my granddaughter lifted her arms up to me again. This time I bent down and I picked her up, and I carried her up to bed, and I did not think about it once until I was back downstairs. Then I sat down at the kitchen table and had a little cry. The good kind.

The sense that I was still myself, still the upright, capable one, and not quietly turning into the small, careful, folded version of my mother that I had watched and dreaded.
