# I Do Not Visit My Friend Joyce as Often as I Should. It Is Not the Drive. It Is What Her Tuesdays Do to Me.

I do not visit my friend Joyce as often as I should.

It is not the drive. The home she lives in now is fifteen minutes from my house, and there is always somewhere to park.

It is what her Tuesdays do to me.

I should start with mine.

I am sixty-four. Two years ago a letter told me my bones were thinning. A T-score of minus 2.6, after six years of taking calcium tablets every single morning. The big chalky ones from the chemist. I did everything I was told, and the letter came anyway.

Since then, the mornings are stiffer. And the little rules have started creeping in without asking. Hold the rail. Mind the wet leaves. Let someone else lift the case.

But I still have my Tuesday.

Tuesday is my day, and it has been for years. The half past nine bus into town. The market, where the fruit man calls me young lady and we both pretend that is normal. Coffee with my sister, the good table by the window if we are quick enough. Then my grandson after school, dropped off at four, demanding the rug and the train track before his coat is even off.

None of it is remarkable. That is the point of it. Nobody arranged it for me. Nobody supervises it. Nobody has to be thanked for allowing it.

And I did not understand what all of that was worth until I saw the other Tuesday.

Because here is what I actually want. Not a number on a scan letter.

I want to be eighty and still have my own front door key in my own pocket.

I want to get down on the rug with my grandchildren, and get back up again without planning it first.

I want to carry my own shopping. Decide for myself what time lunch is. Answer my own front door, at my own speed, to people I invited. Sleep in my own bed, in my own house, with my own things around me. And I want nobody, ever, to speak to me slowly and loudly as if I were a child.

That is the whole dream. Not youth. Just my own life, on my own terms, all the way to the end.

Joyce had all of that.

Five years ago, you could not have told our lives apart. If anything, Joyce was the fitter one. She ran the school office for thirty years and then ran her garden the same way. She walked everywhere. We used to joke that she would outlive the lot of us.

One wet Tuesday, on the pavement outside the post office, Joyce slipped.

One hip.

Hospital first. Then a rehabilitation ward. Then what her daughter calls a lovely room, in a place that is clean, and kind, and is not her house.

This is Joyce's Tuesday now. Lunch is at twelve, whether she is hungry or not. At two o'clock they wheel her to the window. Visitors sign a book in the hallway. There is a young carer who is very sweet and who talks to her slowly and loudly, like you would to a child. And Joyce, who ran a school office for thirty years, lets it happen.

That is the part I cannot watch.

Her daughter drives me past the old house sometimes. Through the side gate you can see the garden doing what gardens do without her. Forty years of runner beans and sweet peas, and this summer it is all grass and bindweed.

Her front door key still hangs on the hook by her old front door. Her daughter has not had the heart to clear the house yet.

Where Joyce lives now, she is not allowed a key.

Ten months ago I sat in my car outside that home for twenty minutes before I could drive off. And I finally admitted what I was actually feeling.

I was not sad for Joyce. Not only that.

I was frightened for me.

Because the difference between my Tuesday and Joyce's Tuesday is one bone. That is all it is. One wet pavement, one bone. And half of all women over fifty will break a bone because of osteoporosis.

I also had to let go of the story I had been telling myself, which is that falls are bad luck. Joyce did not fall badly. She fell the way every one of us has fallen a hundred times in our lives. A slip, a hand out, a bump. The fall was ordinary. What met the pavement was not. The difference was never the luck. It was the bone.

I had a letter in a drawer at home telling me mine were already thinning. And my whole plan was a chalky tablet that had not moved a single number in six years.

That evening I stopped pushing the fear away, and went looking for what actually keeps a woman on the right side of her own front door.

And I found something the professors have been saying for years, in plain sight, that nobody had ever said to me.

The bone specialists do not call calcium tablets a treatment. They call them a top-up. An extra. In study after study, the tablets have not been shown to reliably prevent broken bones. And the same specialists follow it with one sentence that changes everything: get your calcium from food. Because calcium in food arrives with everything it needs to actually be used. Calcium pressed into a chalk tablet arrives alone.

Food first. Not food as well. Food first. It has been sitting in the specialists' guidance for years. Nobody prints it on a tub.

Six years. Every morning. The wrong form.

There is an article that explains it properly, with the sources laid out. It is at the bottom of this post.

What I did after reading it takes less effort than the tablets ever did.

Goat milk from Dutch pasture herds, gently dried at low temperature so the proteins that carry the calcium survive. One ingredient. Nothing else on the label. It is called Goatly. One scoop in my morning tea, ten seconds, and it replaced the tablet, the heavy stomach, and the guilt in one go. It is A2 milk, the kind that sits gently on an older stomach. There is no ritual. Nothing to swallow. It is food, not medicine.

And it comes with a 90-day money-back guarantee, even if the pouch is empty. I had given the tablets six years on trust. This asked me for ninety days, on their risk. That is a different kind of promise.

Before I ordered, I read the reviews, and I noticed something. They were full of women exactly like me, and almost none of them talked about miracles. They talked about stomachs, mornings, and scan letters that had stopped getting worse. That was the language that made me trust it.

It has been ten months. I will tell you what happened in the order it happened, because the order is the honest part.

The first two weeks were about my stomach, of all things. The tablets used to sit in me like a stone through half the morning. That simply stopped. Then the mornings themselves started asking less of me. The getting up. The first trip down the stairs.

The bones took their time, because bones always do. Anyone who promises you a new skeleton in a month is selling you something worse than chalk.

But my last scan letter, for the first time since this began, did not say worse. The mornings are easier than they have been in years. And two weeks ago I got down on the rug with my grandson to fix his train track, and got back up, and only realised in the kitchen afterwards that I had not thought about it once.

I visited Joyce last Tuesday. I read to her, and we laughed about the old head teacher, and it was lovely, and it was hard.

Then I drove home, let myself in with my own key, and stood in my own hallway a moment longer than I needed to.

Two Tuesdays. One bone between them.

I know which one I am fighting for.

## NOTES

**Loop-structuur (pain → relief → pain), de mapping:**
1. Meet her where she is: haar brief (-2.6), zes jaar tabletten, de sluipende regels. Kort, herkenning.
2. Relief, ruim geschilderd: de droom van tachtig zijn met je eigen voordeursleutel, het kleed, de boodschappen, zelf beslissen, nooit toegesproken worden als een kind. Dit is waar ze heen wil, en ze krijgt het 120 woorden lang.
3. Strip it away: Joyce. Fitter dan zij, één natte stoep, één heup, en nu de bezoekersstoel, lunch om twaalf uur, de carer die langzaam en hard praat, en de sleutel die nog aan het haakje hangt bij een huis waar ze nooit meer woont.
4. De pijn is nu scherper dan in de opening, omdat ze de goede versie net heeft gevoeld: "I was frightened for me" + "one bone between them" + de 1-op-2-stat.
5. De grab: het onderbewuste zoekt de dichtstbijzijnde uitweg → professors → food-not-pills → Goatly → en de loop sluit letterlijk: zij draait haar eigen sleutel om en staat in haar eigen gang.

**N.E.S.B. mapping:**
- New: de onafgemaakte professor-zin ("never finished out loud"), food-not-pills als het andere antwoord, "The wrong form."
- Easy: één schep, tien seconden, geen ritueel, niks slikken, "less effort than the tablets ever did".
- Safe: 90 dagen geld terug ook bij lege zak, "food, not medicine", "six years on trust vs ninety days on their risk".
- Big: de eigen voordeursleutel op je tachtigste, je eigen leven op je eigen voorwaarden tot het einde. De hele relief-sectie is de Big.

**Manipulatie-guard (kritiek voor deze avatar):** de doelgroep herkent ads beter dan elke andere leeftijdsgroep (Ofcom 2026) en haat betutteling (79%). Daarom: Joyce ingehouden en concreet (geen melodrama, geen sterfscène), en de vertelster bekent haar eigenbelang ("I was not sad for Joyce. Not only that. I was frightened for me."). Die bekentenis maakt de loop eerlijk in plaats van manipulatief, en is tegelijk de P.I.G.

**Research verwerkt:** invisible-woman (de carer-scène + "nobody speaks to me slowly and loudly", direct uit het gedocumenteerde gat); correcte stat ("half of all women over fifty will break a bone", ROS-framing, 2x); casting-regel (creative 2 toont haar zelf, lo-fi, wat 77% van UK-ads nalaat); geen verworpen cijfers; geen omkeer-claim (payoff = "did not say worse" + het kleed-moment, ervaring); claim-scoping intact (professors-claim = "not been shown to reliably prevent", gedekt door JAMA 2017).

**Roster-plek:** eerste loop-structuur-ad; onafhankelijkheid als hoofdemotie (nieuw naast schuld/UK04v2, generational fear/UK16, bewegingsangst/UK17, schaamte/UK17-Wendy, krimpen/UK18). Joyce = vriendin, geen moeder (geen UK16-botsing). Naam-checks: Christine en Joyce nieuw in het roster; Margaret bewust vermeden (review-naam advertorial), en de eerdere narrator-naam Susan vervangen omdat Susan al als PDP-review-naam draait (óók een heup-verhaal: zelfde fingerprint-fout als Rynse). T-score -2.6 als diagnose uniek. Tijdlijn-fix dubbel-dubbel-pass: automoment "Last month" → "Ten months ago" (matcht "It has been ten months").

**Format-regels:** GEEN mid-ad CTA (alleen eind), "at the bottom of this post"-verwijzing houdt de belofte; 3 P.S.; geen bold/italics in body; geen em-dashes; 👉 + volledige URL; ~1.530 woorden body (verlengd op verzoek Talat 9 jul, was 1.143; toegevoegd: haar eigen Tuesday uitgeschreven met bus/markt/zus/kleinzoon-om-vier-uur die de payoff plant, "answer my own front door" in de relief, Joyce's verwilderde tuin met bindweed, het falls-are-bad-luck-reframe "It was the bone.", "Food first. Not food as well.", reviews-zonder-mirakels, en de tien-maanden-tijdlijn in eerlijke volgorde: maag, ochtenden, dan pas botten). Strip-sectie bewust kort gehouden: de loop werkt op vaart.

**Hook-herschrijf (feedback Talat):** de oorspronkelijke opening ("There are two Tuesdays in this story") kondigde de eigen structuur aan als een verteller: literary writing, geen bekentenis, P.I.G. begraven in alinea 3. "Two Tuesdays. One bone between them." valt nu alleen nog aan het eind, als kristallisatie i.p.v. herhaling van een aangekondigd frame.
