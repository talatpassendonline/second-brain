# I Have Been a Nurse for 38 Years. For the Last Four of Them I Have Refused the Calcium Pills My Own GP Keeps Prescribing Me. Jean Is the Reason. I Was the One Who Rang Her Daughter.

I have been a nurse for thirty-eight years. And for the last four of them I have refused the calcium pills my own GP keeps trying to prescribe me.

I need to tell you why. It starts with Jean, and it starts in a car park.

Jean was seventy-three. Eight years on Calcichew. She took it faithfully, every morning, exactly as prescribed. Her notes said compliant patient. Compliant. As if doing what you are told is the highest thing a woman can achieve.

She was loading her shopping into the car on a Tuesday morning, outside the Tesco on Greenfield Road. Heart attack. She was gone before the ambulance reached her.

I was the one who rang her daughter.

Nobody can tell me it was the tablets. But in four years, nobody has been able to tell me it was not. And after what I have read since, I cannot call it a coincidence any more.

That phone call changed how I looked at my job. I stopped looking at the numbers on the reports. I started looking at what was happening to the people behind them.

Ray was a carpenter. Forty-one years in the trade. Built his own house, then built his daughter's. He had been on calcium tablets and Alendronic acid since he was sixty-five. At sixty-nine his kidney scan came back with small white deposits along the walls. Calcification. When he asked where it came from, his GP said: comes with age.

Dorothy played piano at the care home for thirty years. Everyone knew her hands. She had been on Calcichew since she was fifty-eight. At seventy-one she broke her wrist. At seventy-three, her hip. Her GP said: she is just getting older.

Just getting older. I have heard that sentence more times than I can count. It is what gets said when nobody wants to look any closer.

Then it happened to me. I was sixty. My own scan came back at minus 2.2 and my GP reached for the prescription pad. Calcichew, two a day, plus vitamin D.

"It is what we do," he said. "The benefits outweigh the risks."

I smiled politely, took the prescription, and knew in the car park that I would never hand it in.

Because I finally understood what that sentence meant. The benefit is a number on a report. The risk is a carpenter with white spots in his kidneys and a pianist who cannot play the piano any more.

I was not going to trade mine in.

So I tried to do it myself, properly, the way a nurse would. Magnesium twice a day. Vitamin K2. Boron. Collagen. Strontium, until I read enough about it to stop. Resistance training with the physio on Thursdays. No cheese, then cheese again. I ate so much kale I cannot stand the sight of it.

Three years. The best part of two thousand pounds.

My scans: minus 2.2. Then minus 2.6. Then minus 3.0.

And every time I read a DEXA result out to a patient, I thought about Jean in the Tesco car park.

Then, about three months ago, Brenda came in for her bloods.

I have been taking Brenda's bloods for twelve years. Osteoporosis check-ups. Her score had sat between minus 3.2 and minus 3.5 the entire time. Nothing anyone tried had ever moved it. She had tried everything I had tried, and a few things I had not dared.

That morning she was different. Lighter. She sat straighter in the chair.

Her results had turned around. Inflammation down. Vitamin D in range for the first time in years. Calcium stable, without the Calcichew she had quietly stopped taking. And her GP, for the first time in twelve years, was not talking about starting medication.

I asked what had changed. One scoop of goat milk powder in her morning coffee. Ten weeks.

I want to be honest about my first reaction. In thirty-eight years I have heard every miracle story there is. Shark cartilage. Colloidal silver. Magnet bracelets.

But Brenda is not a miracle person. Brenda is a retired accountant who eats the same breakfast every day and irons her bed sheets. When Brenda tells you something, it is because she has checked it twice.

That night I could not sleep. My last letter said minus 3.0. My GP had brought it up again the week before. Nadine, it has been three years. Please.

At two in the morning I got up, made tea, and opened the laptop.

I typed: why do calcium pills not work for osteoporosis.

I read until it got light outside. And what I found turned thirty-eight years of what I thought I knew on its head.

The calcium in those tablets is ground rock. Calcium carbonate. One mineral, on its own, pressed into a shape.

But bone is not a lump of calcium. Bone is a living building site. It needs calcium, yes. And it needs the whole crew that puts calcium where it belongs: the protein that carries it through your gut wall, the phosphorus and magnesium it gets laid down with, the vitamin K2 that directs it into bone instead of letting it drift.

A tablet has none of that. One mineral, with nobody telling it where to go.

And calcium that nobody directs does not simply leave your body. I read where it can end up. I am not going to print that part here, because I am a nurse, not a scare merchant, and you should read it with the studies next to it instead of my summary of them. It is in the article below, with every source.

Read it sitting down.

👉 It is here if you want it now: https://trygoatly.com/pages/adv2-news-bones

What I will tell you here is the other half. Because the same night, at the same kitchen table, I found what food does differently.

Calcium in milk does not arrive alone. It arrives already attached to a carrier protein, a postman, that walks it through the gut wall and delivers it to the building site. With the phosphorus. With the magnesium. With the K2. The whole crew, in one package, in the amounts bone actually recognises.

Same mineral. Completely different journey. The difference is not the calcium. The difference is everything that travels with it.

There was one more problem, and it was standing in my own fridge.

I had stopped drinking milk five years ago. My daughter and a podcast had convinced me dairy was inflammatory. Oat milk had taken over my coffee. But oat milk has next to no calcium of its own, and the fortified kind is fortified with calcium carbonate. The same crushed rock as the tablet, stirred into a nicer carton.

And the inflammation story turns out to hang on one protein in modern cow's milk. Goat milk does not carry it. Goat milk is A2, the older, gentler protein, and it comes with the full calcium crew intact.

So I ordered goat milk powder the next morning.

The first one was a cheap tub from Amazon. Nine pounds. Two weeks, and I felt nothing. Not a thing. Brenda had turned her whole blood panel around and I could not tell the difference between my scoop and chalk dust.

So I went back and read more, and found the part that nearly made me give up. Most goat milk powder is spray-dried at high heat, because that is cheap. The heat wrecks the very proteins that do the carrying. What is left is the label and the price. I had bought the tablet problem in powder form.

The powder that works has to be dried at low temperature, so the proteins survive. One ingredient, nothing added. From pasture-raised goats, not sheds. And tested by someone independent, so you are not taking the company's word for anything.

The one that passed everything, the one Brenda had been using all along, was a small Dutch brand called Goatly.

I want you to understand what that one bag replaced. The magnesium, the K2, the boron, the collagen, the shelf of separate answers I had been buying for three years. One scoop in my morning coffee. Ten seconds.

When it arrived I could see it was different. Warm white, almost cream, not the grey dust from the first tub. It tastes mild, a little like caramel, and it disappears into coffee.

Three weeks in, I noticed. The constipation I had carried for years was gone. The morning stiffness was less. I stopped waking at four. My hands felt different around the blood pressure cuff.

I wrote it down. Thirty-eight years of filling in charts. Old habits.

At ten weeks I brought my own blood results to my GP.

He looked at the screen. Refreshed it. Looked again. Inflammation down. Vitamin D in range. Calcium stable without a single supplement. Magnesium normal for the first time in seven years.

He was quiet for a long moment. Then he asked me what it was called, and he wrote it down. Not in my notes. On the pad he uses for himself. He tore the page off and put it in his shirt pocket.

Thirty-eight years I have worked alongside GPs. I had never seen one do that.

That was three months ago.

Last week I worked a double shift, did the big shop, cooked dinner, and took my grandson to the woods after school. I did not sit down until nine. I had not managed a day like that since I was fifty-two.

On Sunday my husband looked up from his paper and said: you are humming again. You have not done that in five years.

I had not even noticed.

And then last month, a phone call. Jean's daughter. The one I rang from A&E four years ago.

Her own scan has started slipping. Her GP offered her Calcichew. She rang me, of all people, to ask if she should take it.

I did not answer her with a speech. I sent her a bag of Goatly.

Six weeks later she rang again. Sleeping better. Lighter in the mornings. Her GP happy to wait and watch instead of starting the tablets.

Then she said something I have not stopped thinking about since.

"Mum would have wanted this. You found another way."

I cannot bring a bag to Jean's house. That door is closed. But her daughter has one now. Brenda has one. And you are reading this, so the door is open for you too.
