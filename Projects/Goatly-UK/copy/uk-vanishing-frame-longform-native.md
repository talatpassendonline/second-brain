# For Three Years I Was Getting Shorter and I Blamed My Posture. I Blamed My Shoes. I Blamed Getting Older. I Never Once Blamed What Was Actually Happening to My Spine.

I need to tell you what I got wrong. Because I got the most important thing wrong for thirteen years, and I told my daughter the same lie I told myself.

My mother was five foot six. Same as me.

By the time she was seventy-five she was barely five foot. She had that curve in her upper back that made her look like she was always leaning forward. She hated it. She stopped wanting to be in photographs. She stopped going to her book group because she said the chair hurt her back, but I think it was because she did not want people to see how much she had changed.

I watched it happen. Year by year. Centimetre by centimetre.

And I told myself: that is just what happens. Women get older and they get smaller. It is in the family.

I told my daughter the same thing when she asked about Grandma. "It is just what happens, love." I said it with confidence. I believed it completely.

I was completely wrong.

Here is when it started for me.

Year one. My trousers were too long. I blamed the wash.

Year two. I caught myself in the mirror at Marks and Spencer. Something looked different. I could not place it. I told myself I was tired.

Year three. My daughter said: "Mum, you look different when you stand next to Dad." I told her it was my shoes. Flat shoes, that is all. Nothing to worry about.

Year three, last Tuesday. My daughter stood me against the door frame.

There is a pencil mark there from my fiftieth birthday. She had drawn it as a joke with a ruler and a felt-tip pen. She put the ruler on my head and drew a new line.

Two inches lower.

I asked her to do it again. She did. Same line.

Two inches. Gone.

I am sixty-three years old and I have lost two inches of my own body and I did not notice it happening.

That is not true. I did notice. I noticed the trousers. I noticed the mirror. I noticed my daughter's face when she looked at me next to her father.

I noticed all of it. And every single time, I made up a reason why it was nothing.

The wash. The shoes. The tiredness. Getting older.

Three years of reasons. None of them true.

What was actually happening was my spine. Small bones in my back, compressing. One by one. Quietly. No pain. No warning. No GP appointment. Just a centimetre here, a centimetre there, until the pencil mark on the door frame told me what I had been refusing to hear.

The Royal Osteoporosis Society calls it "silent." That is the word they use. Because bone thins for years without a single symptom. No ache. No sign. Nothing. Until something breaks or something measures shorter than it used to be.

I had never had a scan. I had never broken a bone. I thought that meant I was fine.

It did not mean I was fine. It meant nobody had checked.

After the door frame I went to my GP. I asked for a DEXA scan. She looked at my notes, looked at my age, and said: "Well, yes, it might be worth doing."

As if it were optional. As if two inches of my spine were a maybe.

That night I could not sleep. I went online.

I read the forums. Women writing things like "I have lost three inches and nobody can tell me why" and "is it unusual to lose lots of height" and "my shape had gone."

That last one stopped me. My shape had gone. Because that is exactly what it feels like. Not a medical thing. A you thing. The shape you have had your whole life, just going.

One woman wrote that she felt embarrassed by her abdomen. That her spine curving forward had pushed everything out. She said she had always been proud she never had a pot. Now she did. And it was not from food or drink. It was from her back pushing her organs forward.

She was seventy-six. She sounded heartbroken.

I sat with that for a long time. Because I recognised it. I recognised it in my mother. And I am starting to recognise it in myself.

Then I found something that made me angry. Not sad. Angry.

The Royal Osteoporosis Society. The NHS. The bone specialists. Every single one of them says the same thing:

This is not inevitable. It is not your genes. It is not your destiny.

Bone can be fed. Bone responds to what you give it. The women who lose height are not losing it because they are old. They are losing it because their bones are not getting what they need.

And what they need is not complicated. It is calcium.

But not from a tablet.

The professors who say calcium tablets are useless are right. They have been saying it for years. But the sentence after that, the one nobody ever reads, is: get your calcium from food.

Not a pill. Not a supplement. Food.

Calcium from food arrives with everything it needs to actually reach your bones. Phosphorus. Magnesium. Protein. The full team. A tablet gives you one mineral on its own. Bricks with no builders.

A tablet is the smoke alarm. Food is what puts the fire out.

I had never heard that before. Not from my GP. Not from my mother's GP. Not from anyone.

I started putting one scoop of goat milk powder in my tea every morning. That is it. Not a tablet. Not a routine I have to force myself through. A spoon in my tea. It dissolves and I drink it and that is the whole thing.

I am not going to tell you my two inches came back. They did not. That bone is gone.

But I can tell you this: the stiffness I had every morning, the one I blamed on "just getting older," was gone within two weeks. My daughter measured me again last month. Same door frame. Same felt-tip pen.

Same line.

I wish I could go back and tell my mother. I wish I could go back and un-say "it is just what happens" to my daughter. I cannot do either of those things.

But I can show you the article that changed everything for me. It explains what the professors actually said, why the tablet was never going to work, and what I wish someone had told my mother thirty years ago.
