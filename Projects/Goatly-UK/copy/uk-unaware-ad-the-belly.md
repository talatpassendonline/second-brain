# Same weight I was five years ago. Completely different body. I spent two years blaming the wrong thing.

There is a photo from my sister's sixtieth that I have never shown anyone.

My husband took it from the side while I was talking to someone at the bar. I am wearing the dress I bought specially, the one that looked fine in the shop when I held my breath and stood very straight. In the photo I am not holding my breath. I am just standing.

And the woman in it has a stomach that pushes forward as though she is carrying something. A back that curves higher than it should. A frame that looks compressed, somehow, like someone has gently pressed down on the top of her head. That woman is me. I am ten stone three. I have been ten stone three since I was forty-seven.

I closed the photo. Opened it again. Closed it. Opened it one more time. Then I deleted it.

The next morning I ordered a set of resistance bands and a book about menopause metabolism. Because that was obviously the problem. The menopause had changed where my body stored fat, and I needed to work harder. So I worked harder. For almost two years I worked harder than I had ever worked in my life. I walked five mornings a week. I cut bread, then sugar, then alcohol for three months. I tried intermittent fasting and hated every minute. I did a ten-week pilates course at the leisure centre. Twice.

And the scales stayed at ten stone three, exactly where they had always been. And the belly stayed exactly where it had appeared. And one by one I stopped wearing everything I owned that had a waistband.

That is the part of this story I am least proud of. Not the belly. The two years I spent punishing a body that was not doing what I thought it was doing.

Because here is what I did not understand until much later, the thing that still makes something tighten in my chest when I say it out loud. It was never about the weight. The belly, the rounding, the way my whole outline had changed even though the number on the scales had not. Those things were not fat. And no amount of walking or fasting or pilates was ever going to move them, because I was solving the wrong problem entirely.

What I was actually watching, without knowing it, was the early shape of my mother forming in my own mirror.

My mother spent her seventies getting shorter and rounder. Her back curved. Her stomach pushed forward. She wore big loose jumpers and laughed about it, called herself dumpy, made a joke of the whole thing. But I remember the afternoon she tried on a dress for my cousin's wedding and took it off again without looking in the mirror. I was twenty-three. I did not understand what I was seeing. I understand it now.

I had filed that shape away as something that just happened to women. Gravity, genetics, age. It would not happen to me, I told myself, because I was active and I ate well and I kept my weight steady. And then the photograph happened and it was happening to me anyway, at fifty-four, a good fifteen years earlier than it had started on her.

The thing that cracked the story open was not the belly. It was the pilates class.

There was a woman called Janet who stood next to me every Tuesday. She was sixty-two, eight years older than me. And she had a completely flat stomach. Straight back. The shape of a woman who had never had to negotiate with her own outline. One evening after class I said, half joking, "What is your secret? What do you eat?"

She looked confused. She said, "Nothing special. I just make sure I get enough of the right things every day. Why?"

And I could not answer her, because the answer was: because I eat less than you do and exercise more than you do and my body looks nothing like yours, and I do not understand why. Same age group. Same class. Same effort. Completely different shape.

That small moment stuck. It would not leave.

Six months into the dieting, I had started getting a dull ache between my shoulder blades. Not sharp. Not dramatic. Just a low, nagging soreness that got worse when I walked and did not go away when I sat down. I assumed I had pulled something in pilates. When it was still there three months later I booked a physiotherapist.

She was thorough. She had me stand, bend, twist. And then she did something nobody had done in years. She measured my height.

She paused. She checked her notes against the reading. And she said, very calmly, "Have you measured yourself recently? Because you are almost an inch shorter than your GP record from four years ago."

I said I had not. I said I supposed women just got a bit shorter. It happens.

She said, "It does happen. But not usually an inch in your early fifties." She paused. "Can I ask you something? Your stomach. When did that start?"

I told her. After the menopause, I said. I had been trying to diet it off for two years.

She sat on the edge of her desk and explained something to me in the plainest language I have ever heard, the way I wish every woman was told at forty-five.

She said that after the menopause, when oestrogen drops, the body starts removing bone faster than it replaces it. And the first place that shows is in the spine, because the small bones stacked up your back are the most vulnerable kind. As those bones get a fraction thinner, the spine compresses. Not overnight. A tiny amount at a time, over years. You cannot feel it happening.

But as your spine gets shorter, she said, the space inside your torso gets smaller. And the organs that used to sit comfortably behind your ribcage start pressing forward. The ribcage dips slightly. The abdomen pushes out. The back rounds.

She looked at me and said: "That belly is not fat. Your weight has not changed and you just told me so yourself. What you are looking at is your spine getting shorter and your body rearranging around it. The belly is the inside of your frame, reporting out through the only place it can go."

I did not say anything for a long time.

Because in that moment, two years of effort rearranged themselves completely. The dieting. The fasting. The pilates. The resistance bands I ordered at midnight after staring at a photograph I later deleted. The wardrobe full of loose tops I had quietly trained myself to reach for. I had been fighting the mirror with a calorie app, and the mirror had nothing to do with calories.

And then the second thing hit, the one that made my throat close. My mother. She had not gone dumpy because she stopped trying. Her spine had done exactly what mine was doing, a decade earlier, and nobody had told her what it was, and nobody had told me either. She spent her seventies hiding in jumpers and laughing it off, and the whole time it was her bones, not her body, letting her down.

I asked the physio what I was supposed to do about it.

She said the starting point was feeding the bones properly. She asked me where I was getting my calcium.

I said I took a tablet. Had done for about four years, since the GP mentioned it. Though I forgot it half the time. And when I did take it, it sat in my stomach like a stone.

She nodded like she had heard this a hundred times. She said the guidance from every bone charity in the country was the same: get your calcium from food first, not from a tablet. A tablet drops a large dose of one mineral into the gut all at once, far more than the body can absorb in a sitting. So the surplus just sits there, which is why it felt like a stone, and then it passes through unused. That is not a fault in your body, she said. It is a fault in the delivery.

She said bone is not made from calcium alone. It needs the full team, the phosphorus, the protein, the magnesium, the things that arrive naturally together in food. A lone mineral in a pill is bricks dumped at a building site with no mortar. The bricks just sit there.

She said the simplest thing I could do was find a food-form source of calcium I could genuinely take every single morning for years. Because bone does not respond to what you do for a month. It responds to what you do every day for a very long time.

I went home that evening and did two things. I stepped on the scales. Ten stone three. Then I stood in the hallway against the doorframe where my husband had pencilled our heights when we moved in. The line sat a full inch above my head.

Same weight. Shorter spine. Two numbers, and they told completely different stories. The belly had never been the food.

That week I went looking for the simplest possible way to get real, food-form calcium into my mornings without another tablet I would abandon on the windowsill. What I found, and what I have had every morning since, is a single scoop of pure goat's milk powder stirred into my tea. One ingredient. Just milk, dried. The calcium comes the way it comes in food, in small usable amounts alongside the protein and minerals it naturally arrives with, not as a lump of chalk my gut rejects. It dissolves in thirty seconds. It tastes like slightly sweet milk. It is the easiest thing I have ever added to a morning.

The brand is called Goatly. There is nothing clever in it and that is the entire point. It is the food-first idea the physio pointed me towards, made into something I will actually do every day without having to think about it.

I want to be honest about what happened next, because it was not fireworks and I will not pretend it was.

The first thing I noticed, about two weeks in, was the mornings. That ten-minute window where my back had to be persuaded to straighten got shorter, and then most days it was gone.

By about six weeks, the ache between my shoulder blades, the one that brought me to the physio in the first place, had gone from constant to occasional.

By month three, I bought a pair of trousers with a proper waistband. They sat where trousers are supposed to sit. The belly had not vanished. I am not going to insult you with that claim. But it had stopped pressing forward the way it had been, and something about the way I carried myself had quietly shifted. I stood a bit taller without meaning to.

My husband said it one Saturday. He said, "You look like you again." He could not say what was different. He just said I looked like the person he married. I cried at the kitchen table for ten minutes and then I made my tea with my Goatly in it and carried on with the day.

The moment I think about most is small. I was in the supermarket last month and I reached for a jar on the top shelf without scanning the aisle for someone taller. I only noticed afterwards. I just reached.

I am not writing this to frighten you. But I want to be straight about one thing, because I wish someone had been straight with me three years ago. If your body has changed shape since the menopause and the scales say you have not gained weight, those two facts are trying to tell you something. The belly that no diet will shift is not a failure of willpower. It is a quiet report from a spine that is not getting what it needs. And the window to do something about it does not stay open forever. Bone you lose in your fifties is far harder to rebuild in your seventies. My mother is the proof. I refuse to be the second proof in the same family.

The good news is the window is open today. Your bones are alive and still listening to what you feed them.
