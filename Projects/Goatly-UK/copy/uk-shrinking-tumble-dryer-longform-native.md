# For Over a Year I Blamed the Tumble Dryer. Then a Nurse Measured Me Against the Wall, and I Found Out What Was Really Getting Smaller.

For over a year, I blamed the tumble dryer.

My trousers were coming up long. Dresses I had worn for years suddenly needed a heel. I told my husband the dryer was shrinking everything, and he nodded, because after forty years he knows when not to argue.

Then, on an ordinary Tuesday, I tried on a pair of trousers from the petite rail at M&S. Just to see.

They fit perfectly.

I am not a petite woman. I have been five foot six since I was seventeen. I stood in that changing room, sixty-three years old, holding the label, and I felt something I could not put a name to yet.

The dryer was fine.

I was getting smaller.

That evening I asked my husband, straight out: am I getting shorter?

He looked at me a moment too long. "Maybe a little," he said.

Which, from him, means: yes. Noticeably. For some time.

At my next check-up I asked the nurse to measure me. She stood me against the wall, slid the ruler down, and wrote it in my notes like it was nothing.

Five foot four and a bit. Nearly two inches gone.

And what my GP said next is the reason I am writing this.

He said: "You're just getting older. It happens."

And I nodded. Because when you are a woman of sixty-three, you learn how often you are expected to just nod along.

In the car park afterwards I sat with my hands on the wheel and rehearsed everything I should have said in that room. Two inches is not nothing. You would not tell a man who had lost two inches that it happens. I did not go back in. We never do.

I went home and did what we do. I made tea. I made a joke of it. My mother used to say we come from a long line of shrinkers, and I repeated that line to three different people that week. Every time I said it, my stomach went cold, and I said it anyway.

Because I remembered how it ended. My mother was five foot five in her wedding photos. By her last winter I could rest my chin on the top of her head. Her good wool coat hung on her like it belonged to someone else. And we never once used the word bone in that house. We called it age. We called it one of those things.

Then one evening I stopped joking and typed the question into my phone: why do women shrink as they get older.

I was expecting something comforting. Posture. Gravity. Worn discs.

What I found was the Royal Osteoporosis Society.

In their biggest survey of people living with osteoporosis, 42 percent said the disease had affected them physically. And of those women, 71 percent had lost height or seen their body shape change.

Then came the sentence that stopped me cold. These are warning signs, the charity said, that are often mistaken for just getting old.

There it was. The exact words from the surgery.

Just getting old.

The biggest bone charity in Britain calls it a warning sign. My doctor called it nothing at all.

I kept reading. One study that followed women for ten years found that every centimetre of height a woman loses raises her chance of breaking a bone by around fourteen percent.

Two inches is five centimetres.

I sat with that for a long time.

I did not wait to be offered a scan. I rang and asked for one. It took eleven weeks to happen, and I spent those eleven weeks noticing every kitchen shelf that had quietly become a stretch.

The letter said my T-score was minus 2.5. The exact line where the letters stop saying osteopenia and start saying osteoporosis.

I sat at the kitchen table with that letter and thought about every trouser leg I had folded under and blamed on the dryer.

And here is the part that made me angry instead of frightened.

I had been taking calcium for six years. The big chalky ones from the chemist. Every morning, like a good girl. Six years of tablets, and I had shrunk almost five centimetres anyway.

So I read what the bone professors actually say about those tablets. And the anger got worse.

The specialists do not call a calcium tablet a treatment. They call it a top-up. An extra. Something that was only ever meant to stand behind the real answer. And the real answer, printed in their own advice all these years: get your calcium from food. Because calcium in food arrives with everything it needs to actually be used, and calcium pressed into a chalk tablet does not.

Nobody at the chemist has ever mentioned that. Not once in six years.

Stand in any chemist and look at that shelf. A whole wall of calcium, and the words bone health printed on every single tub. Nowhere does it say what the specialists say, which is that the tablet was only ever meant to be the backup. I had been taking the backup for six years and calling it my plan.

And I will say the part nobody says out loud. Those big tablets sat in my stomach like wet cement. Six years of a heavy, blocked, uncomfortable morning, for a number that got worse anyway. I paid twice: once at the till, once in my stomach.

I am not going to pretend I can explain the whole science here. There is an article that does it properly, the one I wish someone had shown me six years ago. It is at the bottom of this post.

What I did was simple, after six years of complicated.

Goat milk from Dutch pasture herds, gently dried at low temperature so the proteins that carry the calcium survive. One ingredient. Nothing else on the label. It is called Goatly, and it replaced the chalky tablets, and the heavy stomach that came with them, in one go. It is A2 milk, the kind that sits gently even on an older stomach. One scoop in my morning tea. Ten seconds. It stirs in like it was never there and tastes like milk with the faintest sweetness, which surprised me, because I was braced for something that tasted the way the tablets felt.

The first thing that changed had nothing to do with bones. Within a fortnight my stomach felt like mine again. The wet cement feeling was gone, and I only understood how much I had been carrying it when it left.

Bones are slower. Nobody honest will tell you otherwise. So I did the only honest thing I could think of.

I started measuring.

Once a year, on my birthday. Against the kitchen wall, pencil in hand, book flat on my head, my husband checking I am not cheating on my tiptoes.

First birthday: the mark did not move.

Second birthday, this spring: the mark did not move.

Two years. Not another millimetre gone. And my last scan letter, for the first time in years, did not say worse.

I want to be honest with you, because nobody was honest with me. You cannot get the centimetres back. Nobody truthful will promise you that. What you can do is fight for every centimetre you still have. And you can start now, before the petite rail, before the letter, before your husband looks at you a moment too long. The specialists say the same thing in their dry way: the earlier you start, the more there is left to protect. Every year on the wrong plan is a year you do not get back.

Because here is the number that should be on a poster in every GP waiting room: half of all women over fifty will break a bone because of osteoporosis. And for thousands of us, the first warning is not a break. It is a hem that needs taking up. A shelf that got higher. A mark on a wall.

My granddaughter got measured against our kitchen wall last month, the way children always are. Then she insisted on measuring me too.

There are two names on that wall now. Hers keeps moving up.

Mine has stayed exactly where it is for two years. And I intend to keep it there.
