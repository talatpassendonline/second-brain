# For Nineteen Years I Took My Calcium Every Single Morning. Then the Nurse Read My Scan, Went Quiet, and Asked the One Question That Made My Stomach Drop.

"And what have you been taking for it?"

I will never forget the way she asked it. Gently. Carefully. The way you ask someone a question when you already suspect the answer is going to hurt them.

I had been taking it. Every single morning for nineteen years. The calcium tablet, with a glass of water, before my tea. I never missed a day. I was proud of that.

And I watched her face while I told her, and I understood, before she said another word, that the nineteen years had counted for almost nothing.

I am not a careless woman. When my mother's back curved and she got shorter and frightened of her own front step, I swore it would never be me. So I did everything. The calcium, every day. Vitamin D through the winter. More milk, more cheese, more yoghurt. I became the woman who reads every label in Boots.

When my stomach turned on the carbonate ones, I went back to the chemist and they moved me to citrate. When the citrate made me nauseous, I tried chewables. When the chewables tasted like sweetened chalk, I tried the effervescent ones that fizzed in water and tasted like nothing and still sat in my stomach like wet cement. I tried them all. I carried on.

I read the forums at two in the morning. Other women with the same scan, the same tablets, the same GP who said "keep taking it and come back in two years." I was looking for someone who had found the answer. What I found was hundreds of women looking for the same thing.

My daughter noticed before I did. She said, "Mum, you are walking differently." I told her I was fine. She said, "You are holding the bannister now. You never used to hold the bannister." I told her it was nothing.

It was not nothing. I knew it was not nothing. I just did not want her to worry the way I had worried about my mother.

The stiffness in the morning had become so normal I had stopped calling it stiffness. It was just how I got up. Slowly. Carefully. Hands flat on the mattress first. Wait for the back to settle. Then the knees. Then the feet. I thought every woman my age did this. I thought it was just what sixty looked like.

And still the scan came back worse than the one before.

So I did what I think a great many women quietly do. I decided it was me. That my body simply did not absorb it. That it was in the family, and it was coming for me the way it came for her, and there was nothing to be done. I had even read the famous professors saying calcium supplements were useless anyway, a waste of money. And I thought, well, there it is. Even the experts say it is pointless. So I more or less gave up.

I stopped taking the tablets. And honestly? The only thing I noticed was that my stomach felt better. That should have told me something. It took me another year to understand what.

Here is the part I had completely upside down.

Those professors, the ones who said the supplements do nothing, were not talking about calcium. They were talking about the tablet. The artificial, chalky, compressed tablet. And if you read what those exact same experts tell you to do in the very next breath, it is this: get your calcium from food.

That was the sentence nobody had ever finished for me.

Because a calcium tablet was never meant to be the treatment. Look at how the bone specialists themselves describe it and you find a quiet little word. An adjunct. A top-up. A supporting act. I had taken a supporting act and leaned my whole future on it, because nobody ever told me it was only ever meant to stand behind something else.

And the stomach? The heaviness I had lived with for nineteen years? That was the calcium my body could not use, sitting in my gut, passing straight through. My body had been telling me the whole time. I just thought the fault was mine.
